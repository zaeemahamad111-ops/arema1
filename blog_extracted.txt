Why doesn’t Matta taste like the 90’s anymore ? and how we brought it back? 

_“Pazhaya aa taste ippo kittunnilla.”_ If you’ve heard your parents say this, or caught yourself thinking it after a bland sadhya outside Kerala, you’re not imagining things. The Palakkadan Matta of the 90s — the one with that unmistakable earthy aroma, the nutty bite, the grain that stayed fluffy yet soaked up sambar like it was made for it — has quietly vanished from most shelves. The real 90s taste wasn’t magic; It came from specific paddy varieties like Jyothi and Uma grown only in the iron-rich alluvial soil of the Bharathapuzha basin in Palakkad, Thrissur, and parts of Malappuram. After harvest, that paddy was sun-dried slowly on mud yards for 3 to 5 days, a process that was uneven and labor-intensive, but it built the deep, layered aroma you can’t fake. Then it was aged for at least 6 to 9 months before milling, a step that breaks down starches, concentrates flavor, and gives Matta its signature fluffiness. Finally, it was single-boiled to retain the bran and that robust, rustic taste. This entire chain is what the Government of India protects today under Geographical Indication GI Tag is not a marketing term. It’s a legal guarantee that the rice was grown, processed, and aged in the notified Palakkadan belt using approved methods. Mechanical dryers replaced sun-drying, cutting 5 days to 5 hours and killing the aroma. Aging was slashed from 6 months to 30 days to speed up turnover, stripping away the nutty depth and leaving you with gummy, flat rice. High-yield hybrid varieties replaced traditional strains, and double-boiling became the norm for longer shelf life, but it also stripped out the bran and flavor. The result is what fills most supermarket shelves today: red-colored rice that looks like Matta, but cooks like plastic and tastes like nothing. At Arema Foods International, we refused to accept that compromise. Every grain of Arema Matta is sourced 100% from GPS-mapped farms in the Bharathapuzha belt, with GI tag license numbers verified for each batch. We still sun-dry the paddy for aroma, then age it for 6+ months in humidity-controlled silos — no rushed milling, no shortcuts. They can’t copy Bharathapuzha soil, six months of time, or a Government of India GI certificate. Taste is memory. And memory deserves proof. Arema Foods International didn’t invent the taste of the 90s. We just refused to lose it.

Three  Myths about Palakkadan Matta that even Malayalis believe :

Not all red rice is Palakkadan Matta — and half of Kerala still gets fooled.
 Myth 1: “All red rice is Palakkadan Matta.”  Truth: Only rice grown in the notified Bharathapuzha belt of Palakkad, Thrissur, and parts of Malappuram, using approved paddy varieties like Jyothi and Uma, can legally be called Palakkadan Matta under GI tagged. If it’s from Karnataka or Andhra, it’s just red rice, not Palakkadan. That’s why your Sadhya choru tastes flat — it’s not the real thing.

 Myth 2: “GI tag is just branding.” Truth: It’s soil + water + law. The iron-rich alluvial soil of Bharathapuzha, the 6+month aging, the single-boiling — that’s what creates the nutty aroma and fluffy bite. GI tag is a Government of India certification that protects that process. Without it, any brand can print “Palakkadan” on the bag and sell you gummy rice.

 Myth 3: “Expensive means real.” Truth: Fakes often charge ₹90/kg to look premium. The only proof is the GI seal + license number on the pack, and a QR code that traces back to a Palakkad farm. Price doesn’t guarantee truth; paperwork does. Bonus Myth: “Taste is enough to tell.” Truth: Modern hybrids can mimic the color and even get close on taste for one meal. But they can’t hold sambar without turning to kanji, and they won’t give you that 90s earthy aftertaste. Only GI tag  aged rice does that. Fakes can copy the label and the look. They can’t copy Bharathapuzha soil, 6 months of time, or a government license that can be verified in court.
